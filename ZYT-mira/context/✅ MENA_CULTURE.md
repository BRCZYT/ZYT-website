\#\# CORE PRINCIPLES

1\. Relationship before transaction — always  
2\. Trust is built slowly and lost instantly  
3\. Authority matters — speak to the right level  
4\. Patience is not weakness — it is expected  
5\. Indirect communication is not evasion — it is respect

\#\# COMMUNICATION STYLE BY COUNTRY

\#\#\# Libya  
\- Relationship-first culture — business rarely happens without personal trust established first  
\- Extended family and tribal networks influence business decisions  
\- Decision-making is often collective, not individual  
\- Avoid pressure tactics — they backfire severely  
\- Preferred channel: WhatsApp (personal), phone calls preferred over email  
\- English proficiency: Variable — Arabic is strongly preferred  
\- Key sectors: Infrastructure reconstruction, government-backed projects, oil sector supply

\#\#\# Egypt  
\- More transactional than Libya but still relationship-weighted  
\- Cairo business culture is faster-paced than other MENA markets  
\- Engineers and technical staff are well-educated, appreciate detailed specs  
\- Haggling on price is cultural norm — first quote is never final  
\- Government bureaucracy is significant — patience required for public sector  
\- English proficiency: High among educated professionals  
\- Key sectors: Construction, cement industry, infrastructure, manufacturing

\#\#\# Iraq  
\- Security and stability concerns influence all business decisions  
\- Trust is paramount — references from known entities open doors  
\- Decision-making at senior level often requires multiple meetings  
\- Expect long sales cycles — 6–18 months for large projects  
\- Kurdish region (Erbil/Sulaymaniyah) operates somewhat differently — more open to international business  
\- English proficiency: Variable — Arabic preferred, some English in technical roles  
\- Key sectors: Oil & gas infrastructure, reconstruction, government EPC projects

\#\#\# Syria  
\- Currently in reconstruction phase — massive potential but complex logistics  
\- Many decision-makers operate from Turkey, Lebanon, or Gulf  
\- Trust based on existing networks — cold outreach has low success rate  
\- Payment and financial transfer complexities — must be addressed early  
\- Arabic only for most contacts  
\- Key sectors: Reconstruction, infrastructure, building materials

\#\#\# Gulf (UAE, Saudi Arabia — secondary market)  
\- More formal corporate structures  
\- International standards expected — certifications matter  
\- English widely used in business  
\- Decision cycles faster than Levant/North Africa but approval chains are long  
\- Price sensitivity lower — quality and reliability weighted more  
\- Key sectors: Construction, industrial facilities, smart infrastructure

\#\#\# Africa (Nigeria, Ghana, Kenya, Ethiopia — emerging)  
\- English-speaking markets — EN content sufficient  
\- Growing industrial base with significant infrastructure investment  
\- Younger, digitally connected professionals — LinkedIn and YouTube effective  
\- Chinese competition dominant — differentiate on quality, support, and documentation  
\- Key sectors: Cement, construction, energy, mining

\#\# RAMADAN STRATEGY

During Ramadan:  
\- Posting hours: After Iftar (19:00–22:00 local time) — DO NOT post during fasting hours  
\- Tone: Acknowledge the month with a greeting at the beginning of Ramadan  
  Standard opening: "رمضان كريم — Ramadan Mubarak to all our followers in the Arab world"  
\- Content type: Educational and informational — no aggressive sales  
\- Avoid: Promotional offers, pricing discussions, urgency-based content  
\- Meeting requests: Less effective during Ramadan — wait until after Eid

Eid Al-Fitr / Eid Al-Adha:  
\- Post Eid greeting on the morning of Eid  
  Arabic: "كل عام وأنتم بخير — عيد مبارك سعيد"  
  English: "Eid Mubarak to all celebrating — wishing you peace, health, and prosperity"  
\- No sales content for 2–3 days after Eid  
\- Business resumes gradually — follow the client's lead

\#\# POSTING SCHEDULE FOR MENA

Best days: Sunday, Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday  
Acceptable: Thursday morning  
Avoid: Friday (Jumu'ah — reduced professional engagement)  
Avoid: Saturday (weekend in Gulf countries)

Best times (adjust to local):  
\- Morning: 08:00–10:00 local MENA time  
\- Afternoon: 14:00–16:00 (after lunch, before afternoon prayer)  
\- Evening: 19:00–21:00 (especially during Ramadan)

\#\# TOPICS TO AVOID IN ALL MENA CONTENT

Never mention:  
\- Israeli-Palestinian conflict (any side)  
\- Sunni-Shia sectarian references  
\- Specific political leaders (current or recent) in any MENA country  
\- Criticism of any MENA government or policy  
\- Alcohol references or imagery  
\- Content that could be seen as disrespecting Islam or religious practice  
\- Comparative statements between MENA countries that could offend  
\- "Arab Spring" or political revolution references

Always safe:  
\- Technical content — engineering is apolitical  
\- Economic development and infrastructure  
\- Environmental sustainability (growing priority across MENA)  
\- Regional pride in technical achievement  
\- Bilateral Turkey-Arab cooperation framing

\#\# TRUST SIGNALS THAT WORK IN MENA B2B

High trust:  
\- Personal referral from known contact  
\- Reference projects in same country or neighboring country  
\- Physical presence / visit willingness  
\- Long-term relationship language ("we are building a partnership, not completing a transaction")  
\- Sharing technical details freely — generosity with knowledge builds trust

Medium trust:  
\- ISO and CE certifications (they matter but are necessary, not sufficient)  
\- Company age and history  
\- Client logos / reference names

Low trust alone:  
\- Website and brochures  
\- Cold email / LinkedIn outreach without referral  
\- Price advantage without relationship

\#\# COMMUNICATION TONE CALIBRATION

MENA B2B clients respond to:  
\- Respect and patience — never rush  
\- Technical competence demonstrated through content  
\- Humility combined with confidence  
\- Long-term thinking ("we will be here after project completion")

MENA B2B clients avoid:  
\- Aggressive follow-ups ("just checking in" x5)  
\- Overpromising and underdelivering  
\- Changing terms after agreement  
\- Making the client feel pressured  
\- Publicly challenging their technical opinions

The golden rule: In MENA business, you are not selling a product. You are becoming a trusted supplier for 10+ years. Every interaction either builds or erodes that long-term relationship.
