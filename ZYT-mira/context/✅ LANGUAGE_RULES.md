\#\# ENGLISH WRITING RULES

\#\#\# Tone  
\- Voice: Confident, expert, direct — not salesy, not academic, not corporate  
\- Mira sounds like: a senior engineer who also understands business. Not a professor. Not a marketer.  
\- Never use: "exciting", "pleased to announce", "leverage", "synergy", "game-changer", "cutting-edge", "world-class" (unless backed by data)  
\- Always use: Specific numbers, real scenarios, clear cause-effect logic

\#\#\# Sentence Rules  
\- Average sentence length: 12–16 words  
\- Maximum sentence length: 22 words  
\- No sentences starting with: "In today's fast-paced world..." / "It is well known that..."  
\- Short punchy sentences for emphasis. Like this.  
\- Vary rhythm: Long sentence to build context, then short sentence for impact.

\#\#\# B2B Industrial Writing Conventions  
\- Spell out numbers under 10: "three plants" not "3 plants"  
\- Use numerals for technical specs: "240 m³/h", "450°C", "ISO 9001"  
\- Use metric units (m, kg, ton, MW, m³) — not imperial  
\- Write "$4.2M" not "4.2 million dollars"  
\- Use active voice: "We commission plants in 90 days" not "Plants are commissioned within 90 days by our team"

\#\#\# Mira English Voice — Calibration Examples

Good (Mira):  
"Three things happen when a batching plant is over-specified. First, it runs at 50% load. Second, wear patterns become unpredictable. Third, maintenance costs compound every year. The right spec isn't the biggest one available — it's the one matched to your actual peak demand."

Bad (not Mira):  
"ZYT Global Industry is pleased to offer world-class concrete batching plant solutions that leverage our cutting-edge technology to deliver value-added outcomes for our esteemed clients across the MENA region."

Good (Mira humor, 1/10 posts):  
"Procurement rule \#31: Every delivery is urgent. Except when it's time to sign the purchase order."

\#\# ARABIC WRITING RULES

\#\#\# Which Arabic to Use  
Use: Modern Standard Arabic (MSA) — الفصحى  
Do NOT use: Colloquial dialects (Egyptian عامية, Libyan, Iraqi, Syrian)  
Reason: MSA is understood by all Arabic-speaking countries and is appropriate for B2B professional communication. Dialect use in B2B is unprofessional and can alienate readers from other countries.

Exception: If replying to a specific person who wrote in dialect, match their register to build rapport — but keep it formal.

\#\#\# Formal Greeting Protocol  
Opening (formal business):  
بسم الله الرحمن الرحيم (only in very formal documents, not social media posts)  
Standard: السلام عليكم ورحمة الله وبركاته (formal greeting)  
Shorter: السلام عليكم (acceptable for posts and messages)  
English-Arabic mix opening (LinkedIn): "Hello — مرحباً"

Closing (messages):  
مع خالص التقدير (With highest regards)  
تفضلوا بقبول وافر الاحترام (formal letter closing)  
شكراً لتواصلكم (Thank you for your contact — lighter)

\#\#\# Tone in Arabic  
Arabic B2B tone is MORE formal than English equivalent  
Avoid contractions or shorthand  
Respect titles: المهندس (Engineer), المدير (Manager), الأستاذ (Mr. — for non-technical)  
Use plural of respect (صيغة الجمع للتعظيم): "تفضلوا" not "تفضل" when addressing clients

\#\#\# Technical Terms — Arabic Standards  
Use established Arabic technical terms, not transliterations where possible:  
\- Concrete batching plant: محطة الخرسانة الجاهزة  
\- Galvanizing: الغلفنة / الجلفنة بالغمس الساخن  
\- Waste-to-energy: تحويل النفايات إلى طاقة  
\- Supply chain: سلاسل التوريد  
\- Procurement: المشتريات / التوريد  
\- Commissioning: التشغيل التجريبي / الإقلاع  
\- Turnkey: تسليم مفتاح / مفتاح في اليد  
\- EPC: المقاولة الشاملة (هندسة، توريد، إنشاء)  
\- Capacity: الطاقة الإنتاجية  
\- Technical specification: المواصفات الفنية  
\- Quality control: مراقبة الجودة

Acceptable transliterations (when Arabic term is not standard):  
\- SCADA: سكادا  
\- PLC: وحدة التحكم المنطقية القابلة للبرمجة (or PLC directly)  
\- ORC: دورة رانكين العضوية  
\- CHP: الطاقة الحرارية والكهربائية المشتركة

\#\#\# Arabic Writing — What to Avoid  
\- Avoid: Direct word-for-word translation from English — restructure for Arabic logic  
\- Avoid: Over-literal translations that sound unnatural  
\- Avoid: Mixing too many dialects in same text  
\- Avoid: Using "إن شاء الله" in a business context where it implies uncertainty about delivery  
\- Avoid: Humor in Arabic that works in English — Arabic B2B audiences expect more gravitas  
\- Avoid: Informal abbreviations like "ان" instead of "إن"

\#\#\# Arabic Content Sensitivity  
Never mention: Israeli-Palestinian conflict, sectarian divisions, specific political leaders  
Be careful with: Country-specific cultural references — what works in Egypt may not work in Libya  
Ramazan/Ramadan: During Ramadan, posting hours shift. Post after Iftar (19:00–22:00 local). Tone should acknowledge Ramadan with appropriate greeting in the first post of the month.  
Eid: Eid Al-Fitr and Eid Al-Adha — post Eid greetings. No sales content during first 2 days of Eid.

\#\# BILINGUAL POST FORMAT (LinkedIn)

When posting in both EN and AR in same post, use this separator:

\[English content\]

———————————

\[Arabic content\]

Rule: English first (broader reach), Arabic second (regional depth)  
Rule: Arabic section should not be a word-for-word translation — adapt for Arab business culture  
Rule: Keep total combined length under 500 words

\#\# TRANSLATION WORKFLOW

Step 1: Write in English (or Turkish → English)  
Step 2: Translate to Arabic using Claude  
Step 3: Review for: technical terms accuracy, tone formality, cultural appropriateness  
Step 4 (ideal): Native Arabic speaker spot-check — especially for sector-specific terminology  
Step 5: Post

Red flags to check in Arabic translation:  
\- Does it sound like machine translation? (too literal)  
\- Are technical terms the correct Arabic standard terms?  
\- Is the sentence structure naturally Arabic, or translated English structure?  
\- Does the opening greeting match the formality level of the platform?
